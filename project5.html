Molding and casting

    Preparations

    When beginning new projects I usually watch an entire lecture to better understand the material at hand, but this time a good portion of the prerecorded lecture from Fab Academy was of limited use. The random review conducted lasted almost the entire lecture so I skipped though alot of it and instead looked for other resources. The lecture in question can be found here.

    In this project a double negative will be modelled for the Spiral Shell in the Final Project. A step-by-step guide on how the spiral shell was modelled can be found in the final project homepage environment (link). After the negatives are created, a milling simulation will be performed.

    To better understand the concept of double negatives or two-part molds I watched this excellent video on the subject. Thinking that this project would be a piece of cake I zealously embarked on a CAD journey that, unbeknownst to me at the time, would be a lengthy and difficult process.

    Modelling negatives

    I started with creating an offset plane a short distance from the shell.

    Onto that plane I drew a simple square that encapsules the entire body.

    The square was then extruded so that the shell disappeared completely, creating a new intersecting body.

    Under modify the combine modification was opened, the box selected as the target body and the shell as the tool body, and its operation set to cut. Although the picture shows otherwis, "new component" was actually left unchecked but "keep tools" checked.

    It was here that I realised that things would not be so simple. Simply splitting this box in two would not result in a nice two-part mold that could be used for casting. After thinking the problem though for a while the solution was very clear. I drew a new sketch similar to the spiral sketch but going through the circles' centers instead of their endpoints. This would be used as rails for the imminent loft feature.

    The sketch was lofted thusly.

    After repeating the loft feature for all the circles this was the result.

    The resulting body is going to be used as a cutting tool for the box. Like in the first attempt, an offset plane was defined, a square was drawn and it then extruded.

    The combine tool was used just as before.

    The top of the box needed to be slightly cut.

    The result is the bottom part of the two-part mold.

    The top part was simply made by using the loft feature like before but on the inner diameter circles. The result was this.

    A section view of this two-part mold better showcases how the resin will simply fill the empty space once directly poured on the mold.

    The mold is not quite ready yet, the top piece in its current form would simply fall down rendering the mold useless. A simple solution for this is a bracket (new body) that would simply be glued to the top piece, providing stability.

    A small rectangular hole was cut in the bottom piece so that the bracket can snap into it and stabilize the top piece.

    The entire two-part mold assembly looks like this.

    Using the mirror feature a two-part mold for the other side of the shell was made in a heartbeat.

    The next step was to install VCarve and create appropriate toolpaths for the milling of the molds. I got the free trial version from VCarve's homepage. Opening the program, I clicked File and then new. I watched a video on two-sided milling made by Fablab Akureyri here to better understand the user interface.

    The bottom piece of the mold only requires that the job type is set to single sided due to its geometry. The dimensions for the job size were then adjusted to accommodate the entire bottom piece. The resolution was increased a bit and the appearance changed from wood to blue plastic (the material would be wax in reality).

    On the bottom of the gui is a model tab which I selected and then imported the stl. file of the bottom mold piece. After adjusting some of the parameters the model was correctly defined within the job size constraints.

    Pressing "ok" revealed the model.

    A 2D topograph of the design looks like this.

    Next on schedule was to define the rough machining toolpath that would perform the initial milling. I selected an end mill drill bit with a diameter of 6 mm and 2 flutes and adjusted the stepover to 75% as recommended in the video. The spindle speed was set to 12000 rpm and the feed rate to 5000 mm/min.

    After calculating the rough toolpath this was the result.

    The finish mahining toolpath was then defined. Choosing a 3 mm ball nose drill bit with 2 flutes. The stepover rate was greatly reduced to 10% and the feed rate was reduced to 4000 mm/min.

    After calculating the finish toolpath this was the result.
